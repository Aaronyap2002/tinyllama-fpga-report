\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Small-scale large language models (LLMs) such as TinyLlama-1.1B have
emerged as a compelling class of compact transformer models that deliver
meaningful natural language understanding capabilities within a fraction
of the memory footprint of frontier models such as GPT-4. Yet even at
the billion-parameter scale, these models impose substantial computational
demands that limit their deployment in embedded and edge environments.

Currently, these language models are mostly deployed on Graphics Processing
Units (GPUs). However, GPU inference is energy-intensive and
memory-bandwidth-bound, particularly during the autoregressive decode phase,
since the time and memory complexity of self-attention are quadratic in
sequence length.

Field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) offer a compelling alternative to
GPU-based inference by providing reconfigurable hardware with low power
consumption and predictable latency. However, mapping a billion-parameter
transformer to resource-constrained FPGA fabric presents significant
challenges in quantisation, memory tiling, and hardware-software
partitioning.

This project begins with a comprehensive profiling of an RTX~4060 Laptop GPU
running PyTorch inference of the LLaMA~2-based TinyLlama-1.1B model to
identify bottlenecks and measure the proportion of wall-clock time consumed
by the self-attention and feed-forward layers during the forward pass. Based
on these results, an FPGA-based accelerator is proposed to target the
identified bottlenecks.

This project implements and optimises the inference pipeline of TinyLlama-1.1B
on the Xilinx ZCU102 (\texttt{xczu9eg-ffvb1156-2-e}) FPGA platform using
Vitis High-Level Synthesis (HLS). The core transformer components, including
RMSNorm, matrix--vector multiplication, multi-head attention, and the SwiGLU
feed-forward network, were implemented as quantised INT8 HLS kernels.

HLS synthesis of the complete forward kernel on the ZCU102 at a
\SI{150}{\mega\hertz} target clock produces a per-step latency of
1.07--1.37 billion cycles (7.15--9.10 seconds), with resource utilisation of
73\% LUT, 18\% FF, 35\% BRAM, and 28\% DSP, all within the ZCU102 device
budget. C-simulation verified all sixteen kernel primitives against
floating-point software references with zero errors, confirming functional
correctness of the INT8 quantised datapath.
