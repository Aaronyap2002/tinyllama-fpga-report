\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Large language models (LLMs) such as TinyLlama have demonstrated strong
natural language understanding capabilities but require substantial
computational resources that limit their deployment in embedded and
edge environments. Currently, large language models are mostly deployed on 
Graphics Procesing Units (GPU), mostly from Nvidia and AMD, as they offer superior software support 
for Machine Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) researchers and engineers with their respective 
Compute Unified Device Architecture (CUDA) and Radeon Open Compute (ROCM) offerings. 
However, GPU inference is energy-intensive and memory-bandwidth-bound, particularly 
during the autoregressive decode phase since the time and memory complexity
of self-attention are quadratic in sequence length.

Field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) offer a
compelling alternative to GPU-based inference by providing reconfigurable
hardware with low power consumption and predictable latency. But mapping a billion-parameter 
transformer to resource-constrained FPGA fabric presents significant challenges 
in quantization, memory tiling, and hardware-software partitioning.

This project starts off with a comprehensive profiling of a RTX4060 Laptop GPU 
running a pytorch inference of the TinyLlama 1.1B model (based on Meta's Llama 3 architecture)
from HuggingFace with default GPU kernel settings to observe for bottlenecks and 
to measure propotion of wall-clock time required by the self-attention and 
feed-forward layer during the forward pass. Based on the results, an 
FPGA-based accelerator is proposed to accelerate the architecture of the Tinyllama model. 
For this project, emphasis is placed on the hardware implementation and optimization of 
the self attention layer and the feed-forward layer.

This project implements and optimises the inference pipeline of TinyLlama-1.1B
on the Xilinx ZCU102 FPGA platform using Vitis High-Level Synthesis (HLS).
The core transformer components -- including RMSNorm, matrix--vector
multiplication, multi-head attention, and the SwiGLU feed-forward network --
were implemented as quantised INT8 HLS kernels.
